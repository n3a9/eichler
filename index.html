Background

Roots

Entrance to Market Pricing

Eichler homes were never the “mass-produced” housing for the population. When they first entered the market, they were moderately expensive at around a $18,000-$25,000 starting price. At the time, the average home price was around $7,000-$8,000. The high cost of the home was due to the expensive cost of the materials, and building the home. In one recorded conversation, Eichler exclaimed to one of his architects: “I sell these homes for under $10,000 and you can’t design a house for me that I can build for less than $100,000."

Reducing Cost

Eichler, however, was motivated to provide mass, affordable housing for the new, optimistic postwar population. He was not interested in providing only high end homes, but rather to build communities for the families of America. He initially worked extremely close with Bob Ashen, one of his initial lead architects, to go over the design to begin to reduce cost. It took over a hundred major redesigns for the building cost to be brought down enough to be mass produced. It took 5 years, of which the Eichler architects worked tirelessly to continue to redesign the home to reach the market.

Building Efficiency

Not only did cost go down, but building efficiency also greatly increased. It was so time-effective, that some considered it to have “assembly line efficiency." This quick production came at expense, however. Thanks to local construction laws, many aspects of building that had to be sacrificed, down to the bare minimum. They maintained the standard practice of using dry construction in wood frames, for the flexibility and practicality.

Society

Racial Covenants

Minorities, such as African and Asian Americans, accounted for 4% of Eichler sales. Although a small percentage for today, it was a social revolution at the time. Eichler homes were one of the few firms that sold to anyone who could afford: a concept called “open occupancy." Because Eichler homes were taking the step toward a more diverse community, some of the owners from predominantly white community often frowned upon minority neighbors. There was a case where warning signs were made to inform prospective customers that “Eichler homes sells to negroes." Yet, Joseph Eichler strongly believed and continued to practice open occupancy throughout his operations. It was a way to retaliate against the norm that suburbia would be an escape from mixed classes and race. Even with the more diverse communities, there were extremely high levels of community engagement, manifested with the many events and get-togethers.

Morale

The overall morale of the country postwar was optimistic; in California, however, neighborhoods were glowing. The new, young families finally had a chance to settle down and find their happiness and peace, for which they had fought for. This was also manifested in the economy: immediately after WWII, the Californian economy became one of the world’s largest. This allowed for vast and broad neighborhoods throughout California, and specifically populated within the Bay Area.

Design

Modernist Design

'[Eichlers] have a common flavor, but aren't cookie-cutter.' - Peter Schlosser, Architect

Modernist houses often contain large glass windows and atriums, filled with open space and natural light. The idea is carry a sense of the outdoors to the inside of the home, such that nature and the home interior are exchangeable. It crosses a bridge between the norm of suburban life in California, and the growing avant-garde culture. As the design grew and evolved through the times, more standards were set into place about placing of atriums and rooms, as well as formalities such as the kitchen and bathrooms. Even so, modernist design wasn’t meant to be standard – each piece was driven to be unique.

Modernist Roots

Modernism is more than design - it entails social responsibility with the era, evolving with the times. During the Eichler time period, it represented the postwar optimism and a better way of life for new families in suburban California. The designs were purposed for a lifestyle that captured an open society with less segregation, with floor plans that created a flow from room to room. It grew major popularity through promotion efforts postwar. It grew in popularity rapidly due to its purpose being simple and efficient production.

Family Design

Many aspects of the Eichler was built with the family in mind. Joseph Eichler was one of the few architect designers who considered the role of women in the house - the open kitchen engaged the mother with activities and events taking place, while having control of her work. The children’s room is always placed near the atrium for independence while growing up, and adjacent to garden to where children can play privately and safely. To many, living in a California suburbia was meant to protect children, and their families, from the outside.
