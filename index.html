Introduction

To be engaged with architecture is to be engaged with almost everything else as well: culture, society, politics, business, history, family, religion, education." - Paul Goldberger, Why Architecture Matters

Figure 1.0: An Eichler home today.

Question: In what ways did Joseph Eichler's revolutionary architectural design impact social change in the Bay Area after WWII?

Thesis: Despite it starting as a high-end home for upper class families, Eichler homes became one of the most adopted house designs in the Bay Area, and became widely successful in the industry due to its accessibility to anyone, purposeful design, and Joseph Eichler's influence in the house building industry.

Roots

“I sometimes feel that if more fortunate men like me thought as I do, there would be fewer unfortunate people in this world.” – Eichler Homes

Entrance to Market Pricing

Eichler homes were never the “mass-produced” housing for the population. When they first entered the market, they were moderately expensive at around a $18,000-$25,000 starting price.1 At the time, the average home price was around $7,000-$8,000.2 The high cost of the home was due to the expensive cost of the materials, and building the home. In one recorded conversation, Eichler exclaimed to one of his architects: “I sell these homes for under $10,000 and you can’t design a house for me that I can build for less than $100,000.”3

Reducing Cost

Eichler, however, was motivated to provide mass, affordable housing for the new, optimistic postwar population. He was not interested in providing only high end homes, but rather to build communities for the families of America. He initially worked extremely close with Bob Ashen, one of his initial lead architects, to go over the design to begin to reduce cost.4 It took over a hundred major redesigns for the building cost to be brought down enough to be mass produced.5 It took 5 years, of which the Eichler architects worked tirelessly to continue to redesign the home to reach the market.6

Building Efficiency

Not only did cost go down, but building efficiency also greatly increased. It was so time-effective, that some considered it to have “assembly line efficiency.”7 This quick production came at expense, however. Thanks to local construction laws, many aspects of building that had to be sacrificed, down to the bare minimum. They maintained the standard practice of using dry construction in wood frames, for the flexibility and practicality.8

Society

"California design is not a superimposed style, but an answer to present conditions." - Greta Magnusson Grossman

Racial Covenants

Minorities, such as African and Asian Americans, accounted for 4% of Eichler sales.9 Although a small percentage for today, it was a social revolution at the time. Eichler homes were one of the few firms that sold to anyone who could afford: a concept called “open occupancy.”10 Because Eichler homes were taking the step toward a more diverse community, some of the owners from predominantly white community often frowned upon minority neighbors.11 There was a case where warning signs were made to inform prospective customers that “Eichler homes sells to negroes.”12 Yet, Joseph Eichler strongly believed and continued to practice open occupancy throughout his operations.13 It was a way to retaliate against the norm that suburbia would be an escape from mixed classes and race.14 Even with the more diverse communities, there were extremely high levels of community engagement, manifested with the many events and get-togethers.15

Morale

The overall morale of the country postwar was optimistic; in California, however, neighborhoods were glowing. The new, young families finally had a chance to settle down and find their happiness and peace, for which they had fought for.16 This was also manifested in the economy: immediately after WWII, the Californian economy became one of the world’s largest.17 This allowed for vast and broad neighborhoods throughout California, and specifically populated within the Bay Area.18

Design

'[Eichlers] have a common flavor, but aren't cookie-cutter.' - Peter Schlosser, Architect

Modernist Design

Modernist houses often contain large glass windows and atriums, filled with open space and natural light. The idea is carry a sense of the outdoors to the inside of the home, such that nature and the home interior are exchangeable.19 It crosses a bridge between the norm of suburban life in California, and the growing avant-garde culture.20 As the design grew and evolved through the times, more standards were set into place about placing of atriums and rooms, as well as formalities such as the kitchen and bathrooms. Even so, modernist design wasn’t meant to be standard – each piece was driven to be unique.

Modernist Roots

Modernism is more than design - it entails social responsibility with the era, evolving with the times.21 During the Eichler period, it represented the postwar optimism and a better way of life for new families in suburban California.22 The designs were purposed for a lifestyle that captured an open society with less segregation,23 with floor plans that created a flow from room to room.24 It grew major popularity through promotion efforts postwar.25 It grew in popularity rapidly due to its purpose being simple and efficient production.26

Family Design

Many aspects of the Eichler was built with the family in mind. Joseph Eichler was one of the few architect designers who considered the role of women in the house - the open kitchen engaged the mother with activities and events taking place, while having control of her work.27 Postwar, women struggled to find their equality in society – Eichler’s architecture design helped combat this.28 This was also manifested in where the dining table was built. It had the versatility of being able to be put into the dining room or multipurpose room, engaging women as a hostess as well as a mother.29 The children’s room is always placed near the atrium for independence while growing up, and adjacent to garden to where children can play privately and safely.30 To many, living in a California suburbia was meant to protect children, and their families, from the outside.31

Joseph Eichler

Joseph Eichler was concerned about the way people live. Contrary to predictions, he made a success of building houses that faced the future. – A. Quincy Jones: The Oneness of Architecture

Background

Joseph Eichler had a degree from NYU, and owned a family business. Postwar, he quit his job in pursuit of his passion: modern architecture. This was in part due to the home he rented during the war, which was built by Frank Lloyd Wright, another renowned architect.32 This greatly influenced him, and he came to the Bay Area in search of building his own firm. He eventually grew into a team of architects that considered of Eichler Design: Anshen, Steven Allen, A. Quincy Jones, Frederick Emmons, and Claude Oakland, which helped design and build over 11,000 homes throughout their existence.33

Social Values

Joseph Eichler defined standard practice for his firm, in the decisions he made and the values of the company.34 Therefore, all Eichler homes were against racism – Joseph failed to recognize racial covenants, and ensured his subdivisions were free of racism.35 He took this to the length, where he even threatened prospective buyers when they complained about minorities having the privilege to purchase homes.36 In addition, he also believed in second chances. He hired ex-convicts to help build his homes, as a way for them to begin again.37 For this reason, all the communities that he built were socially responsive, and holistic.38
